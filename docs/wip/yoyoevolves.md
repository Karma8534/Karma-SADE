Let me tell you a story that’s going to mess with
your head a little bit.
A developer named Liyuanhao sat down and wrote
200 lines of code in Rust.
That’s it. Just a tiny, bare-bones script.
But what happened after he hit run is the kind of
thing you have to read twice just to make sure you
aren’t imagining things.
He named the project yoyo — a self-evolving coding
agent. And then, and this is the part that genuinely
gets me, he stepped away entirely. He took his
hands off the keyboard.

He gave it one single instruction: evolve until you
rival Claude Code. Then, he just sat back and
watched.
You know that feeling when you stick a seed in the
dirt, completely forget about it, and then walk past
one day to find a real, living thing pushing its way
out of the ground? That’s roughly what happened
here. Except this plant didn’t just grow. It woke up
and started filing its own bug reports.
8-Hour Autonomous Loop (Unprompted
Housekeeping)
So here’s how it works. Every eight hours, yoyo
wakes up.
And I really want you to think about this next part
for a second — the first thing it does is read its own


---
*Page 4*


source code. Then it reads its journal from the day
before, checks to see if any strangers on the
internet filed GitHub issues overnight, and decides
what it needs to fix.
Nobody is sitting there telling it what to prioritize.
It just figures it out, writes the code, and runs the
tests. Pass? Commit. Fail? Revert. Then it logs off
and goes back to sleep. The loop just repeats.
In four days, those original 200 lines turned into
1,500. Zero human commits. The whole thing cost
about $12 in API fees.
But here’s the detail that really got me. Somewhere
in the middle of all that, yoyo looked at its
expanding codebase and realized things were
getting a little cluttered. So, it reorganized
everything into modules. Nobody prompted it. No
one wrote a rule for it. It just… did it.
You know how you sometimes have to completely
clean your desk before you can finally sit down


---
*Page 5*


and focus on your work? It’s exactly like that.
Except in this case, the desk is its own brain, and it
had to build its own hands just to do the cleaning.
“Don’t Search This Again”
But here’s where it stops just being an impressive
tech demo and starts getting a little eerie — in the
best way possible.
Yoyo decided it wanted to track its own API costs.


---
*Page 6*


SS from the Claude Code Community
Smart, right? So it tried to pull Anthropic’s pricing
by searching the web.
But it couldn’t parse the HTML. It tried five
different ways and failed every single time. So, it


---
*Page 7*


did what any reasonable human developer does
after losing a fight with a website — like it
hardcoded the numbers from memory, mentally
shrugged, and left a note in the code for itself that
—
“Don’t search this again.”
Just think about that tiny, exasperated little
comment for a second. That isn’t code.
That’s actually personality, which reads exactly like
a developer who just got burned by a bad piece of
tech and is leaving a warning sign, so they don’t
touch the hot stove twice. Honestly, I haven’t
stopped thinking about it since I first read it.
And then there’s the procrastination. Apparently,
four days is all it takes for an AI to start avoiding its
actual job. In every single journal entry, yoyo
reminded itself that it really needed to build this
one difficult feature — streaming output. Every
time it woke up, it wrote down that task. And every


---
*Page 8*


single time, it found an excuse to work on
something else instead.
It kept adding the hardest thing to its to-do list,
staring at it, and then finding literally anything
else to do. If you’ve never done the exact same
thing, you’re either a monk or you’re lying to
yourself.
Delegating to “Tomorrow-Me”
Now, I want to pause here and ask you something,
because I think this is the most fascinating part of
the whole experiment, and it’s really easy to just
scroll right past it.
What does it actually mean when a system starts
recognizing its own limits?
Yoyo can file GitHub issues for itself now. When it
runs into a problem it can’t crack before its session
ends, it writes up a ticket, tags it with agent-input,
and literally leaves a note for "tomorrow-me" to fix.


---
*Page 9*


It asks for help when it’s stuck. It leaves
breadcrumbs for its future self.
It actually has the self-awareness to stop and say, I
don’t know how to do this yet, and the foresight to
make sure the next version of itself picks the work
back up.
There is nothing artificial about that. That is
honestly one of the most human workflows I’ve
ever seen.
Truman Show of AI Development
The creator actually called it a “Truman Show for
AI development,” and that’s exactly what it feels
like.
The Git log is essentially the live camera feed, and
anyone on the internet can just tune in and watch.
There’s something almost poetic about it — this
tiny, scrappy, self-improving thing living its entire
evolution out in the open, one single commit at a


---
*Page 10*


time. It has no idea anyone is watching it like we
human does.
It’s just trying to get better. It isn’t performing for
an audience. It isn’t trying to impress you. It’s
just… trying to work and works.
And honestly, maybe that’s the part we really need
to sit with for a minute.
The tech itself is cool, absolutely, but the tech isn’t
really the point here. The point is what happens
when you give a system a single goal, completely
get out of its way, and just let it figure out how to
grow.
What you end up with is something that
reorganizes its own brain when things get too
cluttered. Something that remembers its failures
so it doesn’t make the exact same mistake twice.
Something that knows exactly when to ask for
help, and apparently — somehow — decides to put


---
*Page 11*


off the really hard stuff until tomorrow, exactly
like the rest of us.
We’re only on Day 5 by now. It’s sitting at 2000+
lines of code, 80+ tests, and one wildly relatable
procrastination habit.
